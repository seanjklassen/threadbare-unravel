*Positioning, purpose, and messaging hierarchy. Companion to the Brand Style Guide (visual identity, voice, and design language).*

*Version 2.0 — February 2026*

## Brand Strategy

### Why

Making music is one of the most joyful things a person can experience. More people should get to feel that.

But most music software emulates old hardware made by engineers for engineers. Not musicians. The result is tools that reward technical knowledge over emotional intent. There's a gap between feeling something and making something that sounds like how you feel. And it is filled with parameters, menus, and jargon that have nothing to do with music.

Threadbare exists to make that gap as thin as possible.

### How

**Innovative, not emulative.** We don't reproduce classic instruments. We build on their foundations to create things that didn't exist before.

**Immediately understandable.** Every product has one primary control surface that reshapes the entire sound in one gesture. You don't need to understand the architecture to play it. Depth is there when you want it, but it's never in the way.

**Alive by design.** Sound that moves, breathes, and varies — not because we're emulating decay, but because static, perfect sound creates distance between the tool and the person using it. Imperfection makes an instrument feel responsive. Responsive instruments invite play. Play is where the joy lives.

**Modular.** Each product is a focused, complete tool that does one thing well. Like pedals on a board or modules in a rack, they combine in whatever way serves the musician.

### What

Tools for music creation — each one built around a single idea taken further than it's been taken before.

- **Instruments** — generate sound with personality built in.
- **Effects** — reshape sound in ways that respond to what you're actually playing.

Every Threadbare product shares the same interaction model: one surface that gets you 80% of the way, and architecture underneath for the other 20%. Whether it makes the sound or changes the sound, it should feel like the same family of tools.

## Product Positioning

### Waver

**Category:** Instrument

**Position:** A tenderly alive polysynthesizer.

Three voices from different decades — analog poly, consumer FM, divide-down organ — fused into one instrument that drifts, breathes, and never plays the same way twice.

**Proof:**

- Each voice breathes independently — per-voice drift, tolerances, envelope variation
- The puck reshapes the entire instrument in one gesture (presence and age)
- The print chain is part of the voice, not an afterthought
- Every instance has its own personality (Moment), recallable but unique
- Arpeggiator controlled entirely from the puck — no extra controls

**Lineage (for deeper copy, not the headline):** Its voice draws on analog polysynths, consumer FM keyboards, and divide-down organs fused into a single instrument.

**Emotional targets:** Weathered, Tender, Breathing

### Unravel

**Category:** Effect

**Position:** Reverb with a memory problem.

**Proof:**

- Ghost engine weaves fragments of your performance into the tail — the ghost material doesn't decorate the reverb, it *is* the reverb
- Disintegration looper lets sound decay in real time (Basinski-inspired entropy you can control)
- The puck reshapes the space in one gesture (vivid/hazy, recent/distant)
- True stereo — left and right develop independently

**Emotional targets:** Spectral, Wistful, Diffuse

## Messaging Framework

### Hierarchy

Messaging follows a consistent hierarchy from brand to product. Each level answers a different question.


| Level               | Answers                         | Example (Waver)                                                                       |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brand purpose**   | Why does Threadbare exist?      | Making music creation accessible through innovative, immediately understandable tools |
| **Brand method**    | How do we do it?                | Innovative not emulative. Immediately understandable. Alive by design. Modular.       |
| **Product promise** | What does this product deliver? | A synthesizer that sounds tender and alive                                            |
| **Product proof**   | Why should I believe it?        | Per-voice drift, the puck, Moment, the print chain                                    |
| **Lineage**         | What's underneath?              | Analog polysynth, FM keyboard, divide-down organ origins                              |
| **Technical spec**  | What's the DSP?                 | PolyBLEP, OTA filter, OU drift, RBF interpolation, etc.                               |


### Where Each Level Lives

**Brand purpose and method** — About page, investor conversations, brand documentation, interviews. Rarely in product marketing directly. The purpose should be *felt* in the copy, not stated.

**Product promise** — Landing page hero, social media bios, one-line descriptions, store short descriptions. This is the sentence you say when someone asks "what is it?"

**Product proof** — Landing page body, feature sections, demo videos, reviews. The proof makes the promise concrete without becoming a spec sheet.

**Lineage** — Deeper landing page copy, feature descriptions, blog posts, press material. Context for the curious — what instruments influenced the sound, what circuits inspired the design. Never the headline.

**Technical spec** — Store full descriptions, documentation, forum posts, review guides. Dense, accurate, keyword-rich. This audience respects specificity. This is where "PolyBLEP sawtooth," "8x8 FDN," and "Ornstein-Uhlenbeck drift" live.

### Copy Principles

These carry forward from the Brand Style Guide:

**Lead with feel, then function, then mechanism.**
Write the way a musician talks about gear they love. What does it sound like? Then what does it do? Then, only if the audience wants it, how does it work.

*Example: "Waver's voices wander apart over time — each note ages slightly differently, the way a held chord shifts on a real instrument. Underneath, drift models give each oscillator its own trajectory."*

**First person when it adds warmth.**
"We" and "our" in brand-level communication. "I" is acceptable in founder-voice contexts — blog posts, interviews, launch announcements. Never in product UI or spec copy.

*Example (brand): "We build on foundations, not replicas." Example (founder): "I wanted Waver to sound like an amalgamation of the keyboards I grew up playing."*

**Specific over grandiose.**
Name the actual records, the actual synths, the actual experience. Specificity builds trust. Vagueness signals insecurity.

*Example: "The kind of pad you hear on Talk Talk's Spirit of Eden" over "lush, cinematic pads."*

**Acknowledge trade-offs honestly.**
If something is limited, say so. Musicians respect tools that know what they are.

*Example: "Waver's filter is warm but it won't self-oscillate. That's a deliberate choice — we optimized for musical range over extremes."*

**No superlatives, no exclamation marks, no hype.**
The work speaks. If it doesn't, louder copy won't fix it.

**The product name does more work than any descriptor — trust it.**
"Waver" already implies instability and tenderness. "Unravel" already implies decay and transformation. Don't overwrite what the name is doing.

### Describing the Sound

When describing what Threadbare products sound like, prefer language that implies life and responsiveness over language that implies damage and aging:


| Prefer                                 | Over                              |
| -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| alive, breathing, responsive           | aged, worn, degraded              |
| drifts, wanders, moves                 | decays, deteriorates, breaks down |
| tender, gentle, warm                   | broken, damaged, lo-fi            |
| never quite the same twice, unreliable | imprecise, inaccurate             |
| reactive, evocative                    | emulates, models, simulates       |
